# Phase 4.5 — LLM-Reasoned Manipulation: Bridging Force Control and Dispatch

## Goal

Beyond a single grasp's force parameters, the LLM reasons about an object's physical properties *across* a multi-step manipulation sequence — gripping, lifting, carrying, setting down — and that reasoning parameterizes the classical controllers built in Phase 4, rather than the LLM ever directly outputting forces or torques itself. Critically, this isn't purely open-loop: a classical perception monitor watches the held object's actual state during the carry and can trigger a further adaptive response if the LLM's upfront reasoning didn't go far enough. This phase exists specifically to answer the question of whether "intelligence fine-tunes the final steps" can extend beyond the grasp instant to the whole handling sequence — both before the motion (reasoning) and during it (monitoring) — and it sits between Phase 4 (force control) and Phase 6 (LLM dispatch) because it depends on both: it needs Phase 4's controllers to parameterize, and it needs to slot into Phase 6's dispatch flow as the place where object-specific handling judgment gets inserted.

## Why this phase exists, and what question it's actually answering

You asked specifically whether the LLM could take on "lifting and handling," with path planning getting the arm to the object but the LLM's intelligence fine-tuning the final steps. The honest answer, grounded in the actual research literature, splits into a clear yes and a clear no — and this phase is built around being precise about exactly where that line falls, rather than quietly drifting past it.

**The clear yes:** a real, peer-reviewed paper — DeliGrasp (Xie, Valentini, Lavering, Correll, 2024) — built almost exactly this for the grasp moment specifically, and validated it on hardware. An LLM (GPT-4) infers an object's mass, friction coefficient, and compliance from its semantic description alone, and those inferred values parameterize a classical adaptive grasp controller. On a 12-object benchmark of delicate and deformable items spanning two orders of magnitude in mass (a 0.8g paper airplane to a 900g bag of rice), this approach achieved an 80% success rate on minimally-damaging grasps, decisively outperforming classical adaptive grasping baselines, which topped out around 50-55% across the same objects, and dramatically outperforming naive force-limited or vision-only closing strategies (around 28-29%).

**The clear no:** the same paper directly tested the alternative you might be tempted toward — having the LLM estimate the low-level controller parameters itself, directly, rather than reasoning about physical properties first (called "DeliGrasp-Direct" in their own ablation). That direct approach performed worse (70% vs. 80%) and produced more damaging grasps, because it systematically overestimated the force required. This is a controlled, apples-to-apples comparison within the same paper, not a comparison across different studies — it's strong evidence that the LLM should reason about *the world* (what is this object, how heavy, how fragile) and hand that reasoning to a controller, rather than reasoning about *the controller's own internal numbers* directly.

**What's still genuinely open, and what this phase needs to address carefully:** DeliGrasp reasons about one moment — the grasp itself. Your "lifting and handling" framing implies reasoning has to hold up across a *sequence*: grip force needs to account for the *coming* acceleration during lifting, carrying needs to avoid sloshing/tipping for the specific object, setting down needs to avoid impact-related damage. A separate, directly relevant finding is worth taking seriously here: a study specifically probing this exact gap found that LLMs reliably display correct *atomic* world knowledge about individual objects in isolation, but **fail to compose that knowledge into correct solutions for object manipulation and placement tasks** when the task requires chaining several physical facts together correctly. This is precisely the capability your "lifting and handling" framing requires, and it's flagged in the literature as a real, unresolved weak point — not something to assume away because DeliGrasp's single-grasp numbers look strong.

## 4.5a. Architecture: Extending DeliGrasp's Pattern Across a Sequence

### The core structural idea, adapted from PhysObjects

The right reference architecture for going beyond a single grasp comes from PhysObjects (Stanford/Google DeepMind/Princeton): rather than reasoning about an object's properties once, the LLM-based planner *queries* a physically-grounded model about relevant physical concepts *before producing each step of its plan*, validated on a real Franka Emika Panda. The structural shift this represents, relative to DeliGrasp, is treating physical-property reasoning as something the planner can consult repeatedly across a task's steps, not a single property lookup that happens once at grasp time and is then forgotten.

Mapped onto your "lifting and handling" goal: rather than one query producing one set of grasp parameters, the dispatch flow queries physical reasoning at each meaningful transition in the sequence — before gripping (DeliGrasp's existing scope), before the lift begins (does this object's fragility/compliance suggest a slower acceleration profile, or extra grip-force margin to account for inertial slip during the lift), during carrying (does this object's shape/contents suggest orientation constraints, e.g. don't tip a cup), and before setting down (does this object's fragility suggest decelerating into contact rather than a fast release).

### What this is, concretely, in terms of Phase 4's existing controllers

This phase does not add new low-level controllers. It adds a reasoning layer that selects and parameterizes the primitives Phase 4 already built: DeliGrasp-style property inference feeds the grip-force parameter of Phase 4's existing primitives; a similar property-conditioned reasoning step feeds parameters into whichever motion/primitive governs the lift and carry phase (e.g., a slower, more conservative trajectory profile for a fragile or liquid-containing object, versus a faster one for a robust object); and the same reasoning closes the loop at placement, parameterizing Phase 4's guarded-approach primitive's contact-force threshold for a gentler set-down on fragile items.

## 4.5b. What to Actually Build

### Step 1 — Replicate DeliGrasp's grasp-instant reasoning first

Don't start with the full sequence. Get the single-grasp case working exactly as DeliGrasp validated it: an LLM call that takes an object's semantic description and a grasp verb, infers mass/friction/compliance, and parameterizes Phase 4's existing grip-force controller. This is the proven, lowest-risk starting point, and it gives you a working baseline to extend rather than building the harder multi-step version first and discovering the grasp-instant reasoning itself doesn't work in your specific setup.

### Step 2 — Extend reasoning to the lift transition, with a named, concrete mechanism

Add a second reasoning query, triggered specifically at the start of the lift motion, that takes the same inferred object properties (reused from Step 1, not re-queried from scratch) plus the grasp verb/task context, and outputs a parameter for the lift's motion profile. This needs a concrete, named mechanism rather than a vague "slow it down" — there are two real options, applicable at different points in this project's timeline:

- **Available now: MoveIt's velocity/acceleration scaling (Phase 2).** MoveIt's trajectory generation already exposes velocity and acceleration scaling factors per planning request — you can request a slower, gentler trajectory for this specific motion without touching the planner's algorithm at all. The LLM's inferred property (e.g., "full cup of water, high spill risk") maps to a scalar (e.g., "use 40% of max velocity/acceleration for this motion"), which is passed directly into MoveIt's existing scaling parameter. This requires no new infrastructure — it's wiring the LLM's output into a knob Phase 2 already built.
- **Future upgrade, dependent on Phase 4e: MPC cost-function weight adjustment.** If the MPC spine (Phase 4e) is eventually built, NMPC/Contact-Implicit MPC formulations have a cost function with weighted terms — a term penalizing high acceleration/jerk, a term penalizing trajectory deviation, a term for control effort. Increasing the weight on the jerk-penalty term relative to the others makes the *solver itself* prefer smoother trajectories as part of its optimization, rather than clipping velocity after the fact. This is the more sophisticated version of the same idea — the LLM's inferred property would map to a cost-weight adjustment instead of a velocity-scaling factor — but it's gated behind Phase 4e existing, which is currently a stretch goal, not built.

Sequence this as: build and validate the MoveIt-scaling version first, since it's available immediately and doesn't block on anything else in this project; treat the MPC-weight version as a natural upgrade path using the same LLM reasoning, just a different downstream lever, once 4e exists. This is new work either way, not a published, validated pattern the way Step 1 is — treat it as the first real test of whether composing reasoning across steps holds up for your specific tasks, and watch closely for the composition failure mode described above.

### Step 2.5 — Closed-Loop Perception During the Carry: Detecting and Responding to Ongoing Spillage

This is a distinct mechanism from Steps 1-2, worth being precise about the difference: Steps 1-2 are *open-loop* in the sense that the LLM reasons once (or at a few discrete transitions) and commits to parameters before the motion executes. What's being added here is a perception module that *continues watching the held object while the motion is in progress*, and can trigger a further adaptive response if the object's actual observed state (e.g., still visibly sloshing/at risk of spilling) indicates the Step 2 slow-down wasn't sufficient.

**What's actually been done in the literature, and the honest gap between it and your use case.** Closed-loop visual monitoring of liquids in robotic manipulation is real and published — University of Washington's work on visual closed-loop control for pouring demonstrated a robot using deep-learning-based volume estimation fed into a PID controller to pour a target amount, achieving an average 38ml deviation from target, explicitly noted as the first use of raw visual feedback for liquid pouring in robotics. Related work has also fused a chest-mounted camera with a wrist-mounted IMU specifically to monitor an in-progress pour and classify it as succeeding or failing in real time, and separate work used fully convolutional networks for perceiving liquid in a closed visual feedback loop during pouring.

The honest gap: **every one of these is built for the pouring task specifically, not for carrying/transit.** Pouring gives the camera a relatively clean, controlled view of the liquid surface or stream, often from a fixed or slowly-changing vantage point. Monitoring a *moving* cup during a *carry* is a meaningfully different and likely harder perception problem — the camera (whether wrist-mounted or scene-mounted) is observing the cup's exterior more often than a clear view of the water surface, the whole scene is in motion rather than relatively static, and "is this about to spill" has to be inferred from subtler cues (the cup's tilt angle, visible motion/sloshing at the rim if visible at all) rather than a clean volume estimate. This phase is adapting the *closed-loop monitoring pattern* from the pouring literature to a meaningfully different perception target, not dropping in an existing, validated carry-monitoring solution — there isn't one.

**What to actually build, scoped honestly given that gap:**

- Start with the simplest, most tractable signal: cup *tilt angle*, derived from the same 6D pose tracking already built in Phase 3, rather than attempting direct visual liquid-level/sloshing detection as a first pass. Tilt angle is a purely geometric quantity your existing perception stack can already produce, and it's a reasonable proxy for spill risk (a cup tilted past some threshold is at risk regardless of whether you can see the water surface clearly).
- Treat genuine visual liquid/sloshing detection (adapting the FCN- or deep-learning-based approaches from the pouring literature) as a second, harder iteration, only after the tilt-angle proxy is working and you've confirmed it's insufficient for your actual failure cases.
- **The adaptive response, when the monitor flags risk:** route back into the same mechanism named in Step 2 — a further reduction in MoveIt's velocity/acceleration scaling for the remainder of the motion (or, once 4e exists, a further increase in the MPC's jerk-penalty weight) — rather than inventing a new, separate response mechanism. This keeps the system's set of "ways to respond to spill risk" to one well-understood lever, adjusted at two different times (once before the motion via Step 2's LLM reasoning, and again during the motion if Step 2.5's monitor flags continued risk), instead of two different mechanisms that could behave inconsistently with each other.
- **Where the LLM's role sits here, precisely:** the LLM is not the thing doing the real-time monitoring — a continuous LLM call at perception frame-rate is neither the right tool (this is the same "LLMs aren't built for dense, frame-by-frame geometric/continuous estimation" point from Phase 3's perception design) nor necessary. The monitor itself should be the geometric tilt-angle check (or, later, a lighter-weight vision model), running at whatever rate Phase 3's pose tracking already runs at. The LLM's job was already done upstream, at Step 2, when it decided the initial slow-down parameter; this monitor is a classical, fast, deterministic safety check on top of that decision, escalating to a further deterministic adjustment if needed — consistent with this entire phase's core principle that the LLM reasons about the world and hands off to classical mechanisms for anything continuous or safety-critical.

### Step 3 — Extend reasoning to carrying and placement

Following the same pattern: a reasoning query at the start of the carry phase (orientation constraints — don't tip a liquid-containing object; specific to your kitchen task list, this directly matters for the pour task), and one before placement (set-down force/speed). Build these only after Step 2 is validated, for the same reason Phase 4's own sub-phases were sequenced incrementally — don't add reasoning complexity on top of an unvalidated foundation.

### Local model and fine-tuning, via Ollama

Once the reasoning pattern is validated using a capable cloud model (for fast iteration, consistent with the local-first-but-cloud-for-development pattern established earlier), the path to a local Ollama-hosted version follows the same well-documented pipeline DeliGrasp itself used in spirit (they fine-tuned GPT-3.5-Turbo on a physical-properties dataset, PhysObjects, to improve estimates for atypical objects): collect a dataset of property-reasoning examples (potentially generated by the cloud model during development, then used as fine-tuning data — a standard distillation pattern), LoRA fine-tune a compatible local base model (Llama 3.1+, Qwen 2.5+, and Mistral-family models are confirmed to support Ollama's native tool-calling, which this phase needs for clean integration with Phase 6's dispatch), merge the adapters, export to GGUF, and run via Ollama or `llama_ros`. Validate the fine-tuned local model's property estimates against the cloud model's on a held-out set of objects before trusting it in the actual control loop — this mirrors DeliGrasp's own finding that fine-tuning improved estimates for atypical/bespoke objects specifically, which is exactly the case (kitchen objects with unusual shapes/contents) most relevant to your task list.

## 4.5c. Validation: Test the Composition Failure Mode Directly, Don't Assume It Away

Given the literature's explicit finding that LLMs display correct atomic knowledge but fail to compose it across manipulation steps, this phase's testing needs to specifically probe for that failure, not just measure end-to-end success rate and hope composition errors show up as obvious failures.

Concretely: for each task, log the property reasoning's output at *each* step (grasp, lift, carry, placement) independently, and manually check whether each step's reasoning is correct *given* the previous steps' outputs — not just whether the final outcome looked fine. A task can succeed by luck (e.g., the lift-phase reasoning ignored the grasp-phase's inferred fragility and got away with it because the object happened to be robust enough) while still revealing that composition isn't actually working, and that's exactly the kind of failure this validation step needs to catch before it surfaces unpredictably on a different object.

## Risks and Watch-Outs

- **Don't let the LLM estimate controller-internal numbers directly.** DeliGrasp's own ablation shows this performs worse. Always route through "reason about the object/world" → "parameterize an existing classical controller," never "reason about what number the controller needs."
- **Treat multi-step composition as unproven, not as a safe extrapolation from DeliGrasp's single-grasp result.** The literature explicitly flags composition across manipulation steps as a known LLM weak point. Validate each step's reasoning independently (4.5c), not just end-to-end task success.
- **Don't re-query object properties from scratch at every step if they were already inferred earlier in the same task.** Re-querying risks the LLM giving inconsistent answers about the same object across steps (e.g., inferring different mass estimates at grasp time vs. lift time), which would itself be a composition failure. Carry the first inference forward through the sequence.
- **Start with Step 1 (grasp-instant) only, and don't move to Step 2 until it's solid.** This mirrors every other phase's incremental-validation discipline in this project, and is especially important here since Steps 2-2.5-3 are not validated by existing published work the way Step 1 is.
- **Don't expect the carry-monitoring perception (Step 2.5) to work as well as the published pouring-monitoring research it's adapted from.** That research solved a meaningfully easier perception problem (a controlled pour, often a near-static camera view of the liquid) than carry-monitoring (a moving cup, an often-obstructed view of the liquid itself). Start with the tilt-angle proxy, not direct liquid detection, and validate it's actually catching real spill risk before investing in harder visual liquid detection.
- **Keep the LLM out of the continuous monitoring loop itself.** Step 2.5's monitor needs to run at perception frame-rate as a classical geometric/vision check, not as a repeated LLM call — this mirrors Phase 3's reasoning about why dense, continuous estimation isn't an LLM's job. The LLM's contribution is the upstream Step 2 decision; the monitor is a fast, deterministic safety layer on top of it.
- **Route every adaptive response through the same mechanism (velocity/acceleration scaling, or later, MPC weight adjustment), rather than building a second, separate response pathway for "the monitor caught something."** Two different mechanisms for "respond to spill risk" — one decided upfront by the LLM, one triggered reactively by the monitor — could behave inconsistently with each other if they're not the same underlying lever.
- **Validate the local fine-tuned model against the cloud model before trusting it in the control loop**, the same caution DeliGrasp itself applied when comparing fine-tuned vs. base model estimates.

## What "Done" Looks Like

- Step 1 (grasp-instant reasoning) replicates DeliGrasp's pattern: an LLM call infers object mass/friction/compliance from a semantic description, and those values parameterize Phase 4's existing grip-force controller, validated on at least your kitchen object set in sim.
- Step 2 (lift-transition reasoning) is implemented using MoveIt's existing velocity/acceleration scaling as the concrete mechanism, and is specifically tested for composition correctness (4.5c) — not just task success — using the inferred properties from Step 1 without re-querying from scratch.
- Step 2.5 (closed-loop carry monitoring) uses tilt-angle tracking (from Phase 3's existing 6D pose tracking) as its first, simplest signal, and triggers a further reduction in the same velocity/acceleration scaling mechanism from Step 2 when risk is detected — validated by deliberately testing a case where Step 2's initial slow-down is insufficient, confirming the monitor catches it.
- At least one full task (ideally the pour task, since it has a clear orientation constraint during carrying) succeeds with reasoning extended through carry/placement (Step 3), with each step's reasoning logged and manually verified for consistency with prior steps.
- A local Ollama-hosted, fine-tuned model's property estimates are validated against the cloud model's on a held-out object set before being trusted in the actual control loop.
- You can articulate clearly, with reference to DeliGrasp's own ablation, why the LLM reasons about the object rather than the controller's internal parameters, why the continuous monitoring loop is deliberately kept LLM-free, and why multi-step composition is being treated as an open risk rather than an assumed capability.
